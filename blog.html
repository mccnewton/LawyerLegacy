Frequently Asked Questions

Do I Need a Will?

A will is crucial for controlling asset distribution after death. It allows you to name guardians for minor children and appoint an executor. Without a will, state intestacy laws determine asset distribution. Even with trusts, you typically need a "pour-over will" for assets not transferred to the trust.

What's the Difference Between a Will and an Estate Plan?

A will is just one component of a comprehensive estate plan. Estate plans include: wills, trusts, powers of attorney, healthcare directives, and beneficiary designations. Estate plans address both death and incapacitation scenarios.

Should I Create a Trust?

Trusts help avoid probate, maintain privacy, and can minimize estate taxes. Revocable living trusts are most common and can be changed during your lifetime. Trust assets transfer directly to beneficiaries without court involvement. Consider if you have significant assets, want to avoid probate, or need ongoing asset management.

What Happens If I Die Without a Will?

You die "intestate" and state laws determine who inherits your property. Assets typically go to closest relatives (spouse, children, parents) but may not align with your actual wishes. The probate process becomes more complicated and potentially contentious.

What If I Become Incapacitated?

You need durable power of attorney for financial decisions and healthcare proxy/directive for medical decisions. A living will specifies end-of-life care preferences. Without these, family must petition court for guardianship/conservatorship.

Who Should I Choose as My Agent/Trustee?

Select someone trustworthy, responsible, and available. Consider their financial knowledge and ability to handle the role. They should be able to communicate well with beneficiaries. It's often wise to name backup agents/trustees.

How Much Will Estate Planning Cost?

Simple wills: $150-$600. Comprehensive estate plans with trusts: $1,000-$3,000+. Attorney hourly rates: $200-$500. Costs vary by complexity and geographic location. Contact me for a personalized quote based on your specific needs.

How Can I Minimize Estate Taxes?

Lifetime gifting reduces taxable estate value. Irrevocable trusts shield assets from estate taxes. Marital deduction allows unlimited tax-free transfers between spouses. Life insurance provides liquidity for tax payments.

When Should I Update My Estate Plan?

Review every 3-5 years minimum. Update after major life events: marriage, divorce, births, deaths, significant changes in assets or financial situation, moving to a new state, or changes in tax laws.

How Do I Choose the Right Estate Planning Attorney?

Look for specialization in estate planning law. Ask about experience with estates similar to yours. Inquire about fee structure (flat rate vs. hourly). Request references from past clients. Ensure they stay current with tax law changes.

Where Should I Store My Estate Planning Documents?

Keep originals in a secure, accessible location. Inform trusted family members of document locations. Consider safe deposit box for originals, copies at home. Ensure agents/executors can access documents when needed.

What About Digital Assets?

Include digital assets in your estate plan (emails, photos, social media, cryptocurrency). Consider naming a digital executor. Provide access information for important online accounts. Address privacy concerns for sensitive digital information.

How Long Does Estate Planning Take?

Simple estate plans: 2-4 weeks. Complex plans with trusts: 1-3 months. Depends on document complexity and client responsiveness. Initial consultation typically covers goals and basic strategy.

Can I Do Estate Planning Myself?

DIY options exist but carry significant risks. Complex tax and legal issues require professional expertise. Mistakes can be costly and difficult to fix after death. Professional guidance is recommended for most situations.

Do I Need a Lawyer If I Live in Texas?

Texas has specific laws regarding estate planning and probate. Working with a Texas-licensed attorney ensures your documents comply with state law and maximize protection for your family. I'm experienced with Texas estate law and serve the Denton County area.
